Is There a Statewide Dog Poop Law in Missouri?

No. Missouri does not have a statewide "pooper scooper" law. There's no single state statute that says "pick up after your dog." Instead, this is handled at the local level — cities and municipalities each set their own rules.

That means the rules change depending on where you live. What's a non-issue in one zip code could be a fineable offense in the next city over. Here in the St. Louis metro area, most municipalities do have pet waste ordinances on the books.

⚖️ Key Legal Framework

Missouri law does empower municipalities to regulate animals under their general police power. Most local pet waste rules fall under one of three categories: municipal animal control ordinances, public nuisance laws, or property maintenance codes.

Pet Waste Rules by Area: St. Louis City, County & Beyond

City of St. Louis

The City of St. Louis has a clear ordinance requiring dog owners to immediately pick up and properly dispose of pet waste in any public area — sidewalks, parks, streets, and common areas. Residents filed 78 dog waste complaints in a recent 12-month period, and the city does issue citations.

St. Louis County (Unincorporated)

St. Louis County's animal control code (Chapter 611) addresses animal keeping and sanitation. Pet waste that creates unsanitary conditions or nuisances can trigger code enforcement action. The county has the authority to fine and penalize repeat offenders.

What About Your Own Yard?

This is the most common question I hear: "Can I get in trouble for dog poop in my own backyard?"

Generally, no — there's no law requiring you to pick up pet waste on your own private property. But there are exceptions:

  • Public nuisance complaints. If your yard smells so bad the neighbors can smell it, they can file a complaint. Code enforcement can require you to clean up.
  • HOA rules. If you live in a community with an HOA, there are almost certainly pet waste rules in your CC&Rs. HOA fines can be $25–$200+ per violation — and they add up fast.
  • Property maintenance codes. Some cities include pet waste accumulation under their property maintenance standards. Excessive buildup can trigger a violation.
  • Health department action. In extreme cases, accumulated pet waste attracting flies, rodents, or creating unsanitary conditions can trigger health department involvement.

The Berkeley, MO ordinance is one of the strictest — it specifically prohibits "stockpiling pet waste in yard or on concrete surfaces." If your city has a similar rule, your own yard isn't exempt.

Renters, Dog Walkers & Apartment Dwellers

If you're renting, you're still responsible for your dog's waste — both legally and per your lease. Most St. Louis area apartment complexes and rental properties include pet waste clauses:

  • Lease violations for not picking up can result in warnings, fines ($25–$100 per incident), or even lease termination
  • DNA testing is becoming more common at larger complexes — yes, they can match poop to your dog
  • Dog walkers are legally responsible during their care. If you hire a walker, confirm they pick up
  • Common areas in apartments and condos are treated like public spaces under most ordinances

Environmental Law: The MSD Angle

Here's one most people don't know about: the Metropolitan St. Louis Sewer District (MSD) actively campaigns against pet waste because it's a real environmental contaminant.

Dog poop isn't fertilizer. It's the opposite. When it rains, pet waste washes into storm drains and directly into local creeks and streams — untreated. The bacteria, parasites, and excess nutrients pollute waterways, contribute to algae blooms, and can make water unsafe.

While MSD isn't going to fine you directly, their "Don't Be a Party POOPer" campaign underscores that this isn't just a neighborhood etiquette issue — it's an environmental and public health concern recognized by regional authorities.

🌊 The Environmental Impact

A single gram of dog waste contains an estimated 23 million fecal coliform bacteria. In the St. Louis area, stormwater runoff carries pet waste directly into creeks, the Meramec River, and ultimately the Mississippi River — no treatment, no filtering.

What Happens If You Get Caught?

Honestly? Most cities don't have poop police patrolling the streets. Enforcement is almost always complaint-driven. Here's how it typically plays out:

  1. Neighbor files a complaint with city hall or animal control
  2. Code enforcement visits and issues a warning or notice of violation
  3. You're given a deadline to clean up (usually 7–14 days)
  4. If you don't comply, fines start — usually $50–$100 for a first offense
  5. Repeat violations escalate — fines increase, and in rare cases, can reach $500 per incident

In parks and on trails, you're more likely to encounter park rangers or city workers who can issue citations on the spot — especially in popular parks like Queeny Park, Creve Coeur Park, and Forest Park.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it illegal to not pick up dog poop in Missouri?
There's no statewide Missouri law, but most cities and municipalities in the St. Louis area have local ordinances requiring pet waste pickup in public spaces. Fines typically range from $50 to $500.
Can my HOA fine me for not picking up dog poop?
Yes. Most HOAs in St. Louis County include pet waste rules in their covenants. Fines range from $25 to $200+ per violation and are enforceable through your HOA agreement — separate from any city ordinance.
Can I get fined for dog poop in my own yard?
Usually not directly. However, if accumulated waste creates a public nuisance (odors, pests, health hazard), neighbors can file complaints that lead to code enforcement action. Some cities like Berkeley, MO specifically prohibit "stockpiling" pet waste even on your own property.
What should I do if my neighbor won't pick up their dog's poop?
Start by talking to them directly — most people don't realize how bad it's gotten. If that doesn't work, you can file a complaint with your city's code enforcement, animal control, or — if applicable — your HOA. Document the issue with photos and dates.
Can I report someone for not picking up dog poop?
Yes. Contact your city's code enforcement or animal control department. In the City of St. Louis, call the Citizens' Service Bureau at (314) 622-4800 or file online. In St. Louis County municipalities, contact your local city hall.

Spring Cleaning Checklist for St. Louis Dog Owners 🌱🐕

By Jamie, Tidy Tails · March 10, 2026 · 4 min read
Home → Blog → Spring Cleaning Checklist

Spring in St. Louis means one thing for dog owners: it's time to face what winter left behind. Between the freeze-thaw cycles, the rain, and the "I'll get to it next weekend" mentality, most yards in STL County are carrying months of buildup right now.

Here's your no-BS checklist to get your yard back in shape before the first neighborhood cookout.

🐾 Step 1: The Full Yard Waste Sweep

This is the big one. Winter waste doesn't just sit there — it breaks down, spreads, and seeps into your lawn. The longer it sits, the worse it gets for your grass and your family's health.

  • Walk the ENTIRE yard — not just the spots you can see from the deck
  • Check fence lines, behind sheds, and under bushes
  • Remove all accumulated waste before doing any lawn work
  • Consider a professional deep clean if it's been months ($100 one-time from most services)

Pro tip from years of doing this: The spots you think are clean usually aren't. Dogs have favorite corners, and winter hides everything until the snow melts.

🌿 Step 2: Lawn Recovery

Dog waste left over winter creates brown spots and kills grass. Here's how to help your lawn bounce back:

  • Rake out dead grass and thatch from brown spots
  • Apply lime to spots where waste sat (neutralizes acidity)
  • Overseed bare patches — St. Louis lawns do best with tall fescue blends
  • Wait until soil temps hit 50°F+ before seeding (usually mid-March in STL)
  • Water new seed consistently for 2-3 weeks

🏡 Step 3: Set Up a Maintenance Routine

The real move is making sure this doesn't happen again. Once your yard is clean, keeping it clean is way easier than another spring rescue mission.

  • Pick up waste at least weekly (daily is ideal for multi-dog homes)
  • Designate a "go zone" in the yard if possible — easier to maintain
  • Consider a weekly pet waste removal service (starting at $25/week in STL)
  • Set a calendar reminder if you're DIY-ing it

⚠️ Why It Matters More Than You Think

Dog waste isn't just gross — it's a legitimate health risk. The EPA classifies it as a pollutant in the same category as chemical spills. One gram can contain 23 million fecal bacteria. It carries roundworm, E. coli, giardia, and other parasites that can survive in soil for years.

If you have kids playing in the yard, this isn't optional maintenance. It's a health issue.

How Often Should You Pick Up Dog Poop? The Answer May Surprise You

By Jamie, Tidy Tails • March 6, 2026 • 5 min read

Let's be honest — nobody enjoys picking up dog poop. It's the one part of dog ownership that every dog lover silently dreads. So most of us put it off. "I'll get to it this weekend." "It rained, so it'll just... wash away." (It doesn't.)

But how often should you actually be cleaning up your yard? Is once a week enough? Do you need to do it daily? Let's break it down.

The Short Answer: At Least Once a Week

According to veterinarians and the EPA, you should pick up dog poop at least once a week — and more often if you have multiple dogs. Here's why:

  • Parasite eggs become infectious in 2-7 days. Roundworm, hookworm, and other parasites in dog waste need time to become contagious. Weekly cleanup interrupts that cycle.
  • Bacteria multiply rapidly. E. coli, salmonella, and other harmful bacteria in dog feces can survive in soil for months once established.
  • One dog produces about 275 lbs of poop per year. That's a lot of waste accumulating in your yard if you're not staying on top of it.

The Ideal Pickup Schedule by Situation

🐕 One Dog, Average Size

Weekly cleanup is the sweet spot. Your dog produces 2-4 deposits per day, so by the end of the week you're looking at 14-28 piles. Not terrible, but definitely enough to make your yard unpleasant.

🐕🐕 Two or More Dogs

Twice a week or every 3 days. The math gets ugly fast. Two dogs means 28-56 piles per week. That's enough to create genuine no-go zones in your yard. Your kids and guests will notice.

🏡 Small Yard

Every 2-3 days. Less space means higher concentration. A small yard with even one dog can start smelling fast, especially in St. Louis summers when it's 95° and humid.

👶 Kids Play in the Yard

Every 1-2 days — or get a weekly service. Kids touch everything. They roll in the grass. They put their hands in their mouths. If your kids are regularly in the backyard, this isn't optional.

💡 The "Out of Sight, Out of Mind" Trap

Most dog owners underestimate how much waste is actually in their yard because they stop looking. It blends into the grass, gets hidden under leaves, and slowly turns your lawn into a health hazard without you noticing. A professional cleanup often reveals 3-5x more waste than the homeowner expected.

What Happens When You Don't Pick Up Often Enough?

Procrastinating on poop patrol isn't just unpleasant — it creates real problems:

  1. Your grass dies. Dog waste is too acidic and nitrogen-rich for grass. Those brown and yellow patches? That's poop burn. Ironically, the "it's fertilizer" myth makes this worse — dog poop is NOT good fertilizer.
  2. Parasites establish. Roundworm eggs can survive in soil for years. Once your yard is contaminated, just picking up poop isn't enough — the soil itself becomes a risk.
  3. You attract pests. Flies, rats, and other unwanted visitors are attracted to pet waste. More poop = more pests.
  4. Your neighbors notice. The smell carries, especially in warm weather. And if it runs off into storm drains, it's contributing to water pollution.
  5. The job gets worse. 7 piles is a 10-minute job. 30+ piles after a month of neglect is a full afternoon you don't want to spend.
